## What's with the "Bicycle Bling?"

Fox uses "Kashima Coat" to solve for durability issues found with earlier low-friction coatings. Fox's proprietary hard-anodizing process developed by the Miyaki Company in Japan has been around since 2011.

During application, dry lubricating molybdenum disulfide is applied to aluminum stanchions via electrical induction. Fox reserves this distinctive gold treatment exclusively for their top-tier "Factory" series forks, rear shocks, and dropper posts.  You can also find this in MotoGP, supercross, and other sports for its "Diamond-like Coating" (DLC) properties.

Here is a breakdown of what it actually does out on the trail and in the stand.

## The Benefits: Real vs. Marketed

* **Extreme Wear Resistance (The Primary Benefit):** Kashima is essentially a specialized Type III hard anodizing, making it about **four times tougher** than standard anodized aluminum. It is highly resistant to scratches, rock deflections, and general abrasive wear.  

* **Reduced Stiction (The Marketed Benefit):** Fox heavily markets Kashima's ability to lower friction, which theoretically **improves small-bump sensitivity** and responsiveness on rough terrain. However, suspension mechanics generally agree that the actual friction difference between a clean, perfectly lubricated standard black stanchion and a Kashima stanchion is marginal. The "buttery" feel of a Factory fork owes just as much to the high-end SKF seals and premium dampers as it does to the gold coating.  

* **My Opinion:** it sure does *look pretty!*  Only FOX products use Kashima coat.

## Drawbacks and Cost Impact

**The Price Premium:** The biggest drawback is the cost of entry. Because Kashima requires precise manufacturing methods and is outsourced to a single company in Japan, Fox only applies it to their premium Factory line. Upgrading from a Performance series fork (standard black anodizing) to a Factory series fork can cost hundreds of dollars more, even when the air springs are identical.

**Color Inconsistencies:** Because the hard-anodizing process reacts differently depending on slight variations in the aluminum alloy and the chemical bath, the exact shade of gold can vary. A Kashima fork and a Kashima shock on the same bike might not perfectly match in color.  

**Impact on Maintenance:** Kashima does not change your recommended service intervals—you still need to perform 50-hour lower leg services and 200-hour full rebuilds to keep the fork feeling plush. However, it changes how the fork handles wear over time.

**Insurance Against Neglect:** If a rider pushes their service intervals too far, dirt inevitably gets trapped under the dust wipers. On a standard black stanchion, this grit acts like sandpaper and will quickly score the aluminum. Kashima's extreme hardness resists this abrasive wear significantly better, acting as a safeguard for the upper tubes.  

**Long-Term Durability:** For high-mileage riders or those frequently riding in harsh, gritty, or muddy conditions, Kashima **extends the functional lifespan** of the suspension components.  

Ultimately, if you are meticulous about dropping your lowers and replacing your seals on time, standard anodized stanchions will last for years. But if you view suspension service as an annual chore rather than a strict schedule, Kashima is an expensive but highly effective insurance policy against premature wear.

## Fox Model Variations

FOX forks are available in four different “Series”: Rhythm, Performance, Performance Elite, and Factory forks. Higher-end Performance Elite and Factory forks will cost more, but they generally offer more features, lighter weight, and better components.

**Entry Level: FOX Rhythm**
FOX Rhythm forks are original equipment (OEM) forks that only come on complete bikes and are not available to be purchased aftermarket (or on the secondary market). Their GRIP damper is less adjustable, complicated and expensive. The lower legs are  aluminum to keep costs down.

**Mid Level: FOX Performance**
Performance forks use a GRIP damper. Lowers are magnesium for weight reduction. Performance Series forks use black anodized 7000 series aluminum upper tubes as Performance Elite forks. Push-button bleeders may be purchased separately.

**High End: FOX Performance Elite**
Performance Elite forks use higher-end FIT4 or GRIP2 dampers, like Factory forks.  These have the same adjustments, features, dampers, weight, and 7000 series aluminum upper tubes, without the gold Kashima Coat on Factory forks.

**Top-of-the-line: FOX Factory**
Factory is the highest-level FOX fork and it is priced accordingly used by pro teams. All models use FIT4 or GRIP2 dampers. Factory forks are instantly recognizable due to the gold Kashima Coating on the stanchions. Factory forks are also available in FOX's special signature orange color.
