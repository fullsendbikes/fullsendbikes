## Going Deep: Tube Choice

Perhaps the cheapest way to "upgrade" the feel of your bike is through tube choice.  Swapping a typical butyl rubber inner tube with a latex increases comfort, traction and possibly appeal... until you flat.  Tubeless means you never flat again... but needs more maintenance.  TPU tubes weigh almost nothing, making the ride "feel" faster. <br>
What's "right?"

## Marginal Gains

Rolling resistance, the energy lost as tires deform, is the focal measurement in lab testing.  Friction occurs not only within the tire casing, but also between the tire and tube.  Rotating mass is also key. Reducing weight at the outer diameter of a wheel has a greater effect on acceleration and responsiveness than removing weight elsewhere.

Air retention and puncture behaviour are also factors. Some materials hold pressure for weeks; others lose 10–15 PSI overnight. Some holes seal automatically; others demand roadside replacement.

## Butyl: Available Everywhere

Standard butyl tubes (synthetic isobutylene isoprene rubber) remain the most common option due to cost and convenience.  Road tubes weigh 100–125g (equivilent to 4 AA batteries) and [generate rolling resistance](https://www.bicyclerollingresistance.com/) of 13–15 Watts per tire at 80 PSI... much more than latex (-5 Watt) or TPU (-2 Watt).  However, butyl holds air for weeks, can be patched with basic vulcanising cement, and costs between $5 and $10 per tube.

**Butyl is best for commuting, training and prioritizes reliability over marginal performance gains.**

## Latex: Bringing Smiles to Miles since 2008

Natural latex rubber feel and ride faster.

High elasticity and low hysteresis loss reduce rolling resistance figures by 7–8 Watts per tire. Rather than dissipating vibration as heat, latex allows the tire to conform easily, perceived as a smoother ride.

The drawback is porosity. Latex commonly loses 5–15 PSI over 24 hours, requiring inflation before every ride. The material is also vulnerable to pinch flats and tears rather than punctures, sometimes with explosive result at speed.

**Latex works best for time trials and dedicated road racing, where the performance advantage outweighs the maintenance burden.**

## TPU: The New Contender

TPU tubes are made from thermoplastic polyurethane, combining strength with elasticity. The material has matured significantly since early 2020's.

Modern TPU tubes, such as the Schwalbe Aerothan or Pirelli SmarTube, weigh as little as 25–45g for road sizes, *reducing rotating mass by 150–200g* compared to butyl. Rolling resistance falls 7.5 – 9 Watts per tire, closing the gap to latex.

TPU folds to roughly one quarter the size of a butyl tube, making it a compact spare. The material resists punctures from sharp objects more effectively than butyl of similar thickness, per Schwalbe.

However, TPU is prone to installation failures. Improper storage can destroy the material. Repairs also require TPU-specific adhesive patches rather than standard vulcanising cement. Plastic valve stems are also fragile.

**The best TPU inner tubes suit weight-conscious climbers, racers who dislike tubeless maintenance, and anyone who values a minimal saddle bag.**

## Tubeless: For Modern Wheels

Tubeless systems eliminate inner tubes via liquid sealant to create an airtight seal between rim and tire.

By removing tube-to-casing friction, tubeless offers the lowest rolling resistance currently available, recording figures of 6.5–7.5 Watts per tire. The absence of a tube also makes the system much less susceptible to pinch flats, allowing riders to run significantly lower pressures for improved grip and comfort.

Modern high-fiber can seal punctures up to 6mm in real time, often without the rider noticing.

The maintenance burden is the drawback: sealant dries out every 3–6 months (faster in hot climates), and must be replenished. Coagulated clumps can form inside the tire, increasing weight (see above).  *This solution does not work well for older wheelsets* where an airtight seal can not be achieved.

**Tubeless was common for gravel and mountain biking, but has moved to road biking where lower air pressure and increased traction increase performance.**

## Caveats and Gotchas

* It is not recommended to have latex tubes for carbon/rim brake wheels.  Long descents can cause ruptures in the material
* TPU is not advised for carbon/rim brake wheels for the same reason
* CO2 inflation can cause TPU valves to freeze and break
* TPU tubes can deform if inflated outside of a tire, causing "flat spots"
