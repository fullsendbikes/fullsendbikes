## Identify a Worn Cassette

If you ride a bike, you’ve probably noticed that stack of spiky metal gears sitting on your rear wheel. That’s your cassette! It works hard to keep you moving, but just like the tires on your car, it eventually wears down.

Knowing when to swap out your cassette can save you from a frustrating ride — and keep your bike running like new.

## The Big Question: How Often Do You Change It?

There is no magical expiration date for a cassette. How long it lasts depends heavily on how often you ride, the conditions you ride in, and how well you treat your bike. However, a **great rule of thumb is the "three-to-one"** rule.

For every three times you replace your bike chain, you will likely need to replace your cassette. Chains wear out much faster than those metal gears, but a worn-out chain will quickly grind down a perfectly good cassette. Depending on your mileage, a cassette can last **anywhere from 3,000 to 5,000 miles.**

## Signs It’s Time to Say Goodbye

You usually don't need a mechanic to tell you when your cassette is on its last legs. Your bike will let you know.  These are the characteristics of a worn cassette:

* **The Dreaded Skip:** You stand up to pedal hard up a hill, and suddenly the pedals jerk forward with a loud clunk.  This happens because the teeth on the gears have worn down so much that the chain is physically slipping over them under pressure.

* **Sluggish Shifting:** If your gears are taking forever to click into place, or they sound unusually loud and clattery even after a fresh tune-up, a worn cassette might be the culprit.

* **The Shark Tooth:** Take a close look at the metal teeth on your gears.  When they are brand new, the tops are relatively flat and blocky.  When they are totally worn out, they start to look sharp and hooked, exactly like a row of shark teeth (visible at the top of this blog post).

## Pro Tips to Extend Your Cassette's Life

You can't stop wear entirely, but you can slow it down.  Here are a few simple habits to keep your gears spinning happily longer:

* **Keep it sparkling clean:** Dirt, mud, and road grit mix with your chain lube to create a nasty, abrasive paste. It acts just like sandpaper grinding away at your metal gears. Wipe down your chain and cassette regularly, and apply a fresh, light coat of [lubricant](https://www.amazon.com/Dumonde-Tech-Bicycle-Chain-Lubrication/dp/B001ENVJD4/).

* **Shift it around:** We all have that one "favorite gear" we tend to cruise in, but relying on just one or two gears wears them out prematurely while the rest stay untouched. Shift frequently to match the terrain and spread the wear out evenly across the whole cassette.

* **Change your chain early:** As mentioned above, a stretched, old chain is a cassette's worst enemy. Replacing your chain before it gets too worn is the cheapest insurance policy for your cassette.

## Remember your Freehub

It’s important to identify what type of cassette to replace with.  SRAM offers limited options.  Shimano comes in many tooth configurations.  Depending on which brand your drivetrain componentry is, the freehub body style will drastically change. While there is some overlap and cross-compatibility amongst drivetrains, it is best practice to completely match drivetrain componentry. 

Below is an description of each of the modern freehub body and what they look like.

* **HG (Hyper-Glide)** - The most common and traditional freehub body. Created by Shimano, it's the standard for cassettes that have at least an 11-tooth cog as the smallest cog. HG Specific cassettes are available in 8,9,10, and 11speed options.

<img src="https://s3.us-west-1.amazonaws.com/fullsendbikes.com/img/Hub-HG2.webp" alt="Shimano HG Hub">

* **Shimano Micro Spline** - Shimano produces a freehub body design to allow for a wider range 12 speed cassette with a 10-tooth cog. This freehub body is available with 2020+ Shimano SLX, XT, and XTR drivetrains. This unique freehub is only compatible with Shimano produced SLX, XT, and XTR/Micro Spline specific cassettes.

<img src="https://s3.us-west-1.amazonaws.com/fullsendbikes.com/img/Hub-Microspline.webp" alt="Shimano MicroSpline Hub">

* **SRAM XD** - XD was created by SRAM for a larger range of gears, and allows for less than a 11-tooth cog on the cassette. XD is most commonly used for mountain bike applications, but can be applied for gravel, cyclocross, and road bikes as well. XD driver bodies will fit 11 and 12 speed drivetrains with XD specific cassettes.

<img src="https://s3.us-west-1.amazonaws.com/fullsendbikes.com/img/Hub-XD.webp" alt="Campagnolo Hub">

* **SRAM XDR** - (“R” is for Road) is the road version of the XD freehub body.  It looks similar in appearance to the XD freehub body, but is 1.85mm deeper. This is to match the flange spacing for road hubs.  While XDR is road specific, a 1.85mm spacer (Supplied by SRAM) can be used on the XDR freehub body to accommodate XD cassettes.

<img src="https://s3.us-west-1.amazonaws.com/fullsendbikes.com/img/Hub-XDR.webp" alt="Campagnolo Hub">

* **Campagnolo or ‘Campy’** - The Italian answer is one of the more radical in appearance. Campy freehub bodies are sometimes referred to as “CA”, and will fit **8 thru 13 speed** Campy/CA specific cassettes.

<img src="https://s3.us-west-1.amazonaws.com/fullsendbikes.com/img/Hub-Campy.webp" alt="Campagnolo Hub">
