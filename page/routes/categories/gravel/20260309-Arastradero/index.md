## Accessible Mid-peninsula Ride Venue
This after-work gravel loop [around Arastradero](https://www.paloalto.gov/Departments/Community-Services/Parks-Open-Space-Golf-Division/Neighborhood-Parks/Pearson-Arastradero-Preserve) is a golden-hour showcase ride: rolling dirtroads lined with oaks and bay trees, open grassland with sweeping views toward the Santa Cruz Mountains, and quiet ponds where you might spot egrets, red-tailed hawks, or a deer slipping through the brush as the light softens. The perimeter route traces the preserve’s classic ridgeline and valley trails, giving you a mix of mellow traverses, short climbs, and fun descents with plenty of chances to look around and enjoy the landscape instead of just staring at your stem.

## Ride profile and fitness
**Distance:** about 5.9 miles of mixed dirt and gravel trail. <br>
**Elevation:** roughly 700 feet of total ascent and descent, with several short, punchy climbs and flowing descents. <br>
**Terrain:** wide fire roads and smoother singletrack-style sections, a few loose or rutted spots, but nothing overly technical for a confident beginner. <br>
For beginner riders, being comfortable riding 60–90 minutes on rolling terrain and shifting smoothly on hills will make this ride enjoyable.  For intermediate riders, expect a solid tempo effort with a few chances to push harder on the climbs and regroup at the tops.

## Expected fitness requirements
You should be able to ride 8–12 miles on mixed surfaces without long breaks.
You should be able to climb for 3–5 minutes at a time at a conversational pace, and repeat that a few times.
Descending comfort: okay with moderate-speed dirt descents, braking before corners, and riding in light dusk conditions with lights on. <br>
A good rule of thumb: if you can ride an hour on local paved hills without stopping often, you’re in the right fitness range for this loop.

## Suggested equipment
**Bike:** gravel bike or front-suspension mountain bike with 38–50 mm tires and decent tread. <br>
**Lights:** one bright front light (at least “I’d use this on a dark bike path” bright) and a solid or flashing rear light for the ride home and shaded sections. <br>
**Helmet and gloves:** a properly fitted helmet and full-finger gloves for grip and light protection if you tip over. <br>
**Hydration:** 1–2 water bottles or a small hydration pack; aim for at least 20–24 oz on a typical after-work ride. <br>
**Clothing:** breathable jersey or top, layered so you can handle cooling temps after sunset; light wind shell if it might get breezy. <br>
**Spares and tools:** tube or tubeless plug kit, pump or CO₂, tire levers, and a small multi-tool. <br>
**Extras:** small snacks (chews, bar), ID, phone, and a little cash or card. <br>

## Trail etiquette around others

To keep hikers and equestrians happy and welcome bikes back: <br>
**Yield the trail:** always slow or stop for hikers and give horses the right of way. <br>
**Communicate early:** call out a friendly “Hi, two riders behind you” from a distance; with horses, speak calmly so the animal knows you’re a person. <br>
**Control your speed:** slow way down when passing other users and around blind corners; no skidding, and stay on your side of the trail. <br>
**Respect the preserve:** stay on designated trails, do not cut corners, and avoid muddy off-trail lines that create ruts. <br>
**Be courteous:** smile, say thank you when people step aside, and keep group size and noise reasonable, especially in the evening. <br>

This loop is a great way to unwind after work, build fitness, and share the trails with the community in a way that makes everyone glad bikes are there. <br>
